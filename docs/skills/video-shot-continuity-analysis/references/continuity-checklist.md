# Continuity Checklist

## Shot-To-Shot Review

Review the prior shot's ending frame and the proposed next shot against this checklist:

1. Subject continuity
   - Same face, hair, costume, age read, and body build
   - Same facial structure and proportion read, not just same colors and hairstyle
2. Established element continuity
   - Any previously visible companion, prop, creature, effect, vehicle, or crowd still makes sense
   - If something is no longer visible, the crop or exit is clearly motivated
   - If a companion is unstable in an insert shot, reduce visibility instead of letting it distort
3. Environment continuity
   - Same scene layout
   - Same visible landmarks unless the cut intentionally excludes them
   - Similar lighting direction and time-of-day read unless a story beat changes them
4. Coverage continuity
   - Next shot is a believable push-in, pull-back, insert, or alternate angle from the prior frame
   - Adjacent shots should not stay in nearly the same frontal setup unless there is a strong dramatic reason
   - Claimed angle changes must still read correctly even without the prompt text
   - If the shot is still in the same camera family, prefer editing from the prior ending frame instead of rebuilding the scene
   - If the shot claims a real side or off-axis angle, require a camera-angle workflow rather than a simple frame edit
   - Inside one beat, vary shot function instead of stacking repeated hero framings
   - After multiple close reactions or inserts, add a reset shot to restore shared space
5. Axis continuity
   - The screen direction and camera side do not flip accidentally
6. Action-phase continuity
   - Motion does not jump to a disconnected pose
   - A stop beat must inherit the momentum of the prior moving frame before settling
   - If the first shot is moving, the next shot should usually continue that motion briefly before changing state
7. Spatial relationship continuity
   - Attached or interacting elements keep believable front/back order
   - Leashes, straps, weapons, bags, and hands remain on the correct side
   - Occlusion and tension direction still make physical sense
8. Narrative priority continuity
   - Separate missing details into:
     - `must_keep_story_detail`
     - `compressible_story_detail`
     - `optional_flavor_detail`
   - Missing `must_keep_story_detail` means the shot or sequence is not narratively usable
   - Missing `compressible_story_detail` is acceptable if the beat still reads clearly
   - Missing `optional_flavor_detail` should not trigger regeneration by itself

## Story Detail Priority Rules

Treat details as mandatory only when they carry the plot.

`must_keep_story_detail` includes:

- identity reveal that later dialogue depends on
- who rescued whom
- whether the rescue succeeded or failed
- promises, deals, accusations, refusals, or key emotional turns
- any reveal that motivates the next scene

`compressible_story_detail` includes:

- a non-essential intermediate gesture before dialogue
- exact posture transitions that do not change the meaning
- extra reaction beats between two already clear states

`optional_flavor_detail` includes:

- soot wiping, tear wiping, dust brushing, hair adjustment, and similar embellishments when they do not change the beat
- decorative environmental business
- descriptive flourish that enriches the moment but is not required for comprehension

Use this rule in practice:

- If the audience can still understand the same plot beat, the same relationship state, and the same next-line logic, the omitted detail is probably not mandatory.
- If the omitted detail breaks why the next line happens, it was mandatory.

## Crop Rules

Cropping out prior elements is allowed only when all are true:

- The new framing choice clearly motivates the crop
- The cropped-out element is not narratively mandatory to remain visible
- The cut still feels like a tighter coverage choice rather than a scene reset
- The body pose, eyeline, or remaining composition still implies the missing element where needed

Example:
- A companion or prop visible in a wide shot may leave the frame in a close-up only if the cut clearly reads as a push-in and the retained posture or framing still supports continuity.

## Bridge Shot Rules

Use a bridge shot when two neighboring shots would otherwise feel too similar.

Useful bridge shot types:

- Face close-up
- Eye detail
- Hand or prop detail
- Feet or leg movement
- Leash, paw, or shadow detail
- Over-shoulder shift
- True side angle with perspective change
- Very short hinge shot that redirects attention before the next reaction shot

For unstable companions or props:

- Prefer leash, paw, shadow, hand, or body-part detail over a distorted partial face
- Keep only the parts needed to preserve interaction logic

Example:
- Wide walking shot
- Footstep insert continuing the walk
- Closer reaction shot as the character slows and starts thinking

## Bad Example

Previous shot:
- Character walking through a populated street
- Several visual elements are clearly established in frame
- Medium-wide frontal tracking setup

Bad next shot:
- Nice half-body portrait
- Prior elements vanish without explanation
- Environment becomes cleaner or emptier
- Coverage is too similar to the previous shot to justify a cut
- Character face drifts to a look-alike variant
- Claimed as direct continuation

Why it fails:
- The image may be attractive, but it reads as a reset, not a cut from the previous shot.
- It may also waste regeneration budget on visual polish while still missing a required story beat.

## Better Example

Previous shot:
- Character moving through a specific environment
- Multiple visual elements are established
- Motion direction is clear

Better next shot:
- Slightly tighter shot or true alternate angle
- Or an insert/detail shot that bridges the action
- Same scene geometry still readable
- Some prior elements may be cropped, but their absence is motivated and still logically implied
- Companion visibility is reduced intelligently if full visibility causes drift
- The edit method matches the goal: frame edit for inherited coverage, camera-angle workflow for real new viewpoint
- Shot order alternates function, such as relationship -> reaction -> insert -> reaction -> reset
- Facial expression shifts while preserving the action beat from the previous frame
- The action transition happens inside the shot instead of between shots

Why it works:
- It feels like coverage of the same moment rather than a newly invented isolated still.
- It preserves the details that matter to the next beat while allowing non-essential flourish to simplify.
